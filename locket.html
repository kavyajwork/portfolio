Context

Locket lets you share real-time photos
directly on someone's home screen.

Locket is a widget app designed for close relationships — you and your person send each other candid photos that live right on each other's lock screen. No feed, no likes, no algorithm. Just you, them, and a tiny window into each other's day.

It's a beautiful concept. But when I actually used it — and watched others use it — I kept running into friction that the concept didn't deserve. The experience felt fragmented. Notifications were noisy. Sending a photo took more taps than it should. The widget itself was passive, not interactive.

This project started as a class assignment. It became something I genuinely cared about finishing well.

Who we're designing for

People who want to feel close
without the pressure of performing closeness.

Locket's real users aren't posting for an audience — they're trying to close a distance. After interviews and affinity mapping, two distinct users emerged.

Persona 01
Aanya, 23 — the long-distance girlfriend
"I want to feel like I'm in his day, not just texting about it." She sends photos impulsively — while laughing, eating, or bored on the metro. She hates when apps make spontaneity feel effortful. Notification fatigue is a real issue for her.
Persona 02
Rohan, 28 — the elder sibling, abroad
"I don't need to talk every day, I just want to know they're okay." He checks Locket passively — the widget is his window. He wants context at a glance, not another app demanding his attention. He almost never sends, but always looks.

I also ran a heuristic evaluation against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and a competitive audit of BeReal, Snapchat, and Widgetsmith. The audit revealed that Locket's widget was uniquely passive compared to competitors — it showed photos but offered no interaction from the lock screen itself.

Honest moment
I initially assumed the core problem was visual design — that the widget "looked too plain." My research completely proved me wrong. Users loved the look. The friction was entirely in the interaction model. Good reminder: don't design for what you assume, design for what you find.
Key problems

Three friction points that were quietly
killing the magic.

Problem 01
Sending a photo is a 6-step journey
Open app → tap camera → take photo → preview → confirm → send. By step 3, the spontaneous moment has passed. Users described it as "too much effort for something that should feel casual."
Problem 02
Notification overload leading to opt-out
Every photo triggered the same generic push notification. Users in active groups were getting 15–20 a day. 67% of surveyed users had turned off notifications entirely — meaning they missed moments they would have cared about.
Problem 03
The widget is passive, not interactive
The lock screen widget shows a photo — but that's all it does. You can't react, reply, or even see who sent it without unlocking and opening the full app. For passive users like Rohan, this broke the entire value proposition.
Constraint worth noting
iOS widget limitations are real
iOS Live Activities and WidgetKit have strict interaction constraints. Any solution needed to work within Apple's system — no custom gestures, no arbitrary animations. This shaped every design decision in solutions 3 and 4.
Solutions

Four features. One throughline:
less friction, more presence.

Feature 01 · Sending

Quick-capture shortcut — from 6 steps to 2.

A persistent camera shortcut on the Locket home screen lets users take and send a photo in two taps: open app → camera fills the screen → tap the shutter. No preview step unless you want it.

The key decision was removing the mandatory preview screen and making it opt-in. Users who wanted to review before sending could swipe up. Users who wanted to just send, could.

UX reasoning
Based on interview data, 78% of users said they "almost always send the first take." The preview step existed for quality control, but real user behaviour showed it was used as a friction point, not a feature. Removing the mandatory step and replacing it with an optional swipe gesture follows Fitts' Law — the most common action gets the shortest path.

Feature 02 · Notifications

Smart notification grouping — signal over noise.

Instead of one push per photo, Locket batches notifications from the same sender within a 30-minute window. A summary notification reads: "3 new photos from Priya" with the most recent visible as a thumbnail preview.

Users can set a "quiet hours" window per person — so Rohan can receive Mom's photos silently during work, but still see them the moment he unlocks his phone.

UX reasoning
67% of users had disabled all Locket notifications — which means they were missing the core value entirely. Notification grouping follows the principle of reducing interruptions while preserving information. The per-person quiet hours feature was directly requested in 5 of 8 interviews, making it a high-confidence addition rather than a design assumption.

Feature 03 · Widget interaction

An interactive lock screen widget — react without unlocking.

Using iOS Live Activities, the redesigned Locket widget lets users send a quick reaction directly from the lock screen: a single tap reveals 4 emoji reactions, and a long-press opens a text reply field.

This was the most technically constrained feature. iOS WidgetKit doesn't support arbitrary tap interactions — Live Activities does, but only for specific use cases. I worked through this constraint in collaboration with a developer to propose something feasible within Apple's guidelines.

UX reasoning
The lock screen widget is where passive users like Rohan spend most of their Locket time — looking, not sending. Enabling a reaction from the lock screen removes the single biggest drop-off point in the passive user journey. The 4-emoji constraint (not unlimited) was deliberate: too many options at lock screen level creates decision fatigue. Four is enough to feel expressive without requiring thought.

Feature 04 · Relationship context

A "presence ribbon" — ambient awareness, not surveillance.

A soft, optional feature: a tiny coloured dot appears on your person's widget when they've been active in the last hour. Not their location, not their exact activity — just a gentle signal that they're around.

I debated this one a lot. It could easily tip from "warm" to "invasive." The answer was making it entirely optional, both to show and to see — and defaulting it to off.

UX reasoning
Research showed that the underlying anxiety for many users wasn't "did they see my photo?" but "are they okay right now?" The presence ribbon addresses that emotional need without requiring the other person to actively perform availability. Privacy-by-default (off until both users opt in) was non-negotiable, and was designed explicitly to avoid the surveillance patterns that have damaged trust in apps like Snapchat's Snap Map.
The "oops" moment
During usability testing, participant 3 immediately said "this feels like tracking." That feedback stopped me cold — and led to a full redesign of the feature's onboarding copy and opt-in flow. The feature didn't change. The framing completely did. This was the most valuable 20 minutes of the entire project.
Usability testing

5 sessions. 3 critical fixes.
1 completely unexpected insight.

I ran 5 moderated usability sessions using a ProtoPie prototype. Participants were asked to complete 3 tasks: send a photo, respond to a notification, and interact with the lock screen widget. Sessions were recorded and transcribed.

Finding 01
The quick-capture button was mistaken for a "profile" button by 2 participants due to its circular shape.
→ Changed to a camera icon with a subtle shutter animation on hover.
Finding 02
The "quiet hours" toggle was buried in settings — 4 of 5 participants didn't find it without prompting.
→ Surfaced as a contextual option within the notification itself ("Quiet this person for now?").
Finding 03
The presence ribbon felt "stalker-y" to one participant — the language "active recently" felt surveillance-like.
→ Renamed to "around" with a warmer description. Full opt-in redesigned as a mutual agreement flow.
Surprise insight
Participants asked for a feature I hadn't designed: seeing the exact moment a photo was taken, not just when it was sent. "It matters if they took this at 2am — that tells me something."
→ Added as a low-priority feature to the backlog. Would explore in a V2.
Challenges

The hard parts were mostly about
not designing what I wanted to design.

The biggest challenge wasn't finding solutions — it was resisting the urge to add features just because they were technically possible. I generated 40+ concepts in ideation and had to kill most of them.

The iOS constraint on widget interactions was a genuine design constraint I hadn't encountered before. Understanding WidgetKit vs. Live Activities vs. Dynamic Island required real technical research. I spent a full week just mapping out what was and wasn't possible before touching Figma.

The emotional stakes were also unusual. Locket exists in the space of relationships — love, longing, family. Designing the wrong thing here doesn't just create a bad experience. It could actively damage something that matters to someone. That weight informed every decision.

Learnings

What I'd carry into every project
after this one.

01
Research invalidates your best assumptions.
I was convinced the widget looked too plain. Users didn't care about the visual at all — they cared about the interaction. If I'd designed from assumption, I would have solved the wrong problem beautifully.
02
Framing is a design decision.
The presence ribbon feature wasn't changed by usability testing — its wording was. "Active recently" vs. "around" is a 7-character difference that changes how the feature feels entirely. Copy is UX.
03
Technical constraints sharpen creativity.
The iOS widget limitations forced me to design a more considered solution than I would have if I had unlimited freedom. Constraints are not the enemy of good design — unconstrained design is.
04
The "oops" moment is where the learning lives.
My biggest growth in this project came from the 20 minutes after participant 3 said "this feels like tracking." I didn't get defensive — I got curious. That's the skill I'm most proud of building here.
Final outcome

Four features. A redesigned mental model.
And a much cleaner case for connection.

The redesigned Locket experience reduces the sending flow from 6 steps to 2, surfaces notification control at the point of need, enables lock-screen interaction within iOS constraints, and introduces ambient presence as an opt-in feature — all without compromising the app's core promise of intimacy over performance.

If I were taking this further, I'd want to run a longitudinal study on how the presence ribbon affects relationship satisfaction over time. The feature introduces a new social dynamic that deserves more than a 5-session usability test to evaluate properly.
